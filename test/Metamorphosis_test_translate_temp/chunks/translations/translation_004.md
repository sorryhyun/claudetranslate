## Translation Notes

### Major Translation Decisions

1. **Gregor's internal rationalization**: The opening paragraph shows Gregor's self-deception—"아직은 그런 일들에 대해 걱정할 필요가 없었다" (There was no need to worry about things like that yet). He's minimizing the crisis, treating his inability to admit the chief clerk as a "사소한 무례함" (minor discourtesy). This cognitive dissonance is central to Kafka's absurdism—treating the impossible as merely inconvenient.

2. **Chief clerk's formal condescension**: His speech (lines 18-39) required careful calibration in Korean to convey institutional authority with personal disapproval:
   - "바리케이드를 치고" (barricade yourself) - militaristic accusation
   - "전혀 들어본 적 없는 방식으로" (in a way that is quite unheard of) - formal shock
   - "이상한 변덕을 부리는 것처럼 보이는군요" (seem to be showing off with peculiar whims) - condescending surprise
   - "명예를 걸고 말씀드릴 뻔했습니다" (came near to giving him my word of honour) - formal pledge showing Gregor's compromised trustworthiness
   - The shift from using Gregor's name to accusatory "당신" (you) marks increasing coldness

3. **Money accusation**: The chief clerk's reference to "최근에 당신에게 맡겨진 돈" (money recently entrusted to you) introduces suspicion of embezzlement—darkly ironic given Gregor's total dedication to paying off family debts. Korean "명예를 걸고" (word of honour) emphasizes the gravity of financial trust.

4. **Gregor's rambling defense**: His response (lines 41-57) shows psychological breakdown beneath polite surface. The Korean captures his frantic, incoherent reassurances:
   - "자신도 잊어버리고" (beside himself and forgetting all else) - loss of self-control
   - Rapid, contradictory statements: "침대에 누워 있습니다" (still in bed) then "막 침대에서 나오고 있습니다" (just getting out of bed)
   - Excessive deference: "제발, 부모님을 괴롭히지 마세요" (Please, don't make my parents suffer)
   - Desperate work commitment: "8시 기차를 타고 출발하겠습니다" (I'll set off with the eight o'clock train)
   - The honorific speech level remains extremely high despite his panic, showing ingrained workplace submission

5. **Physical struggle sequence**: The attempt to stand upright (lines 59-75) uses clinical, precise language matching Kafka's style:
   - "매끄러운 서랍장 위로 올라가려고 했지만 그냥 다시 미끄러져 내려왔다" (tried to climb up on the smooth chest of drawers he just slid down again)
   - "마지막 한 번의 힘을 주어" (gave himself one last swing)
   - "심각한 고통을 느꼈지만 더 이상 그것에 주의를 기울이지 않았다" (in serious pain but he no longer gave any attention to it)
   - "작은 다리들로 가장자리를 꽉 잡았다" (held tightly to the edges with his little legs)—insect body matter-of-factly described

6. **The pivotal revelation**: "저것은 동물의 목소리였어요" (That was the voice of an animal)—this is the moment Gregor's transformation becomes undeniably apparent to others. The chief clerk's clinical observation uses formal speech ("~었어요") with eerie calm, contrasting violently with the mother's "비명" (screams). The Korean term "동물의 목소리" preserves the shocking directness without specifying what kind of animal.

7. **Family's cascading panic**: The final paragraph shows rapid escalation through fragmented dialogue:
   - Mother's horrified realization: "심각하게 아플 수도 있는데" (he could be seriously ill)
   - Desperate calls across rooms: "그레테! 그레테!" / "엄마?"
   - Father's commands with hand-clapping: "지금 당장 자물쇠 수리공을 불러!"
   - Emergency action: "두 소녀는, 치마를 휘날리며, 즉시 홀을 통해 뛰쳐나갔고"
   - The Korean captures the chaotic urgency through short, staccato phrases

8. **Sensory details**:
   - Visual: "치마를 휘날리며" (skirts swishing)
   - Auditory: "문이 쾅 닫히는 소리는 들리지 않았다" (no sound of the door banging shut)
   - The absence of the expected sound (door closing) creates unsettling silence

9. **Symbolic open door**: The final observation "무언가 끔찍한 일이 일어난 집에서는 사람들이 종종 그렇게 한다" (people often do in homes where something awful has happened) uses general cultural observation to mark this as a recognized crisis moment. The locked door that kept Gregor trapped has given way to an open door left in panicked flight—barrier broken but not in the way Gregor hoped.

10. **Gregor's conditional logic**: His thought "만약 그들이 충격을 받는다면 더 이상 그레고르의 책임이 아닐 것이고" (If they were shocked then it would no longer be Gregor's responsibility)—he's still trying to rationalize, to find a scenario where his transformation is someone else's problem. Darkly comic hopelessness.

### Kafkaesque Elements Preserved

1. **Bureaucratic dehumanization**: The chief clerk's long speech treats Gregor as case file, not human being. His concerns are institutional (turnover figures, business duties, employer's interests) not personal. The Korean workplace vocabulary ("매출", "업무상 의무", "사업상의 고려") emphasizes this cold professionalism.

2. **Communication failure**: Gregor speaks extensively (41-57) but isn't understood. The chief clerk's question "당신은 그 말을 한마디라도 이해했습니까?" (Did you understand a word of all that?) shows complete communication breakdown. What Gregor thinks is human speech registers as animal sounds.

3. **Physical entrapment as metaphor**: Gregor's struggle to climb the smooth chest of drawers, sliding down repeatedly, mirrors his inability to rise to the demands placed on him. The "매끄러운" (smooth) surface offers no purchase—like trying to climb social/professional hierarchy with insect body.

4. **Absurdist priorities**: Even transformed into insect, Gregor worries about:
   - Eight o'clock train schedule
   - Recommending himself to the boss
   - Proving he's not making excuses
   - His recent sales figures
   - The contracts he sent in
   His priorities are completely misaligned with reality—tragicomic essence of Kafka.

5. **Authority's arbitrary power**: The chief clerk's personal visit for one absence, the immediate suspicion of embezzlement, the threat that Gregor's position is "not all that secure"—all show how precarious employment is. Korean readers familiar with hierarchical workplace culture will recognize this anxiety.

6. **Family as audience**: The locked door creates theater—family and chief clerk as audience to Gregor's invisible performance. They hear sounds, voice, but can't see. The revelation comes through sound ("동물의 목소리") not sight, delaying but intensifying horror.

### Cultural Resonance for Korean Readers

1. **Workplace hierarchy and anxiety**: The immediate suspicion at absence, the senior manager's personal visit, the threat to job security, the accusation of shirking duty—all resonate deeply with Korean workplace culture's hierarchical pressure and expectations.

2. **Family obligation and shame**: Gregor's desperate concern "제발, 부모님을 괴롭히지 마세요" (Please, don't make my parents suffer!) aligns with Korean 효 (filial piety) values. His transformation brings shame to family, threatening their financial security and social standing.
